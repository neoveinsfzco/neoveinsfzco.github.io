2: I get no results or irrelevant results when I search PubMed with a phrase. What should I do next?

Phrase Searching with Double Quotes


If PubMed’s Automatic Term Mapping (ATM) does not process a phrase in a way that gives you relevant results, you have another option.

Let’s say you want to find citations that discuss the Healthy Brain Study from Emory University. You go to PubMed and search for healthy brain study. The results don’t appear to discuss the study we are looking for.


A review of the Search Details reveals that PubMed processed each term individually.


This happens because the phrase does not map to a MeSH term, so the words are processed only as individual terms.

If this happens, you may want to bypass ATM and search for a specific phrase. First, try searching with the phrase enclosed in double quotes: "healthy brain study".


Using this strategy, most of the results refer to the Healthy Brain Study:


Phrase Index

When you enclose a phrase in double quotes, PubMed will bypass ATM and look for it in the phrase index. The PubMed phrase index is a collection of phrases that appear in PubMed. Read more about the phrase index in the PubMed Help Guide.


This search works because Healthy Brain Study is in the phrase index.

What is in the Phrase Index?

You can see what is in the phrase index by following these steps:

  1. Go to the PubMed Advanced Search Builder.
  2. Type your phrase into the search box.
  3. Click the "Show Index" button.
  4. If your phrase appears, select it from the drop-down menu. The number beside the phrase indicates approximately how many results that phrase will return.


When you search PubMed for a phrase enclosed in double quotes, if your quoted phrase is in the phrase index, it will be searched, which you can see in the Search Details:


You’ll notice that because the exact phrase was found in the phrase index, PubMed did not break apart your phrase and search for the individual terms.

The section "Phrase Searching with a Proximity of Zero" will explain what to do if your phrase is not in the PubMed phrase index.

Important Note

Enclosing a phrase in double quotes will only search for that exact phrase. For example, searching for "genetic counselor" does not search for the plural form ("genetic counselors"). The next section of this tutorial demonstrates how to use wildcards in a word within a phrase to look for alternate spellings.


Key Takeaway

When you enclose a phrase in double quotes, PubMed will look for it in its phrase index. If there is a match, PubMed will search the exact phrase in all fields. This bypasses and turns off Automatic Term Mapping (ATM), and your search will not include term variations.
